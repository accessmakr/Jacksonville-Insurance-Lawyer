What Is Insurance Bad Faith?

When you purchase an insurance policy, you enter into a legally binding contract. Embedded within this contract is an implied duty of "good faith and fair dealing." This means your insurer is legally obligated to investigate, negotiate, and settle your claims honestly and promptly.

Legal Meaning

In Florida, insurance bad faith is an actionable tort. If an insurance company prioritizes its profits over its contractual obligations to you—such as denying a covered claim without cause or intentionally lowballing a settlement—they are acting in bad faith. By learning how to sue an insurance company, policyholders can hold these corporations accountable and potentially recover damages well beyond the original policy limits.

What It Means & Examples

What Is a Bad Faith Insurance Claim?

A bad faith claim is a separate legal action brought against your insurer. It is not just a dispute over how much money you should receive for a car accident or property damage. It is a lawsuit alleging the company engaged in deceptive, unfair, or illegal practices while handling your initial claim.

Examples of Bad Faith by Insurance Companies

Insurers employ numerous tactics to minimize payouts. Common examples include:

  • Denying a valid claim: Rejecting coverage for a peril explicitly covered in the policy without a valid reason.
  • Delaying a claim: Ignoring communications or stalling an investigation to pressure the policyholder into dropping the issue.
  • Underpaying a claim: Offering a settlement drastically lower than the documented damages.
  • Failing to investigate: Denying a claim without conducting a thorough, independent investigation.

First-Party vs Third-Party Bad Faith

There are two primary categories of bad faith:

  • First-Party: Your own insurance company refuses to pay a legitimate claim you filed on your policy (e.g., your homeowner's insurance denying roof damage).
  • Third-Party: Your liability insurer fails to defend you or settle a claim brought against you by another person, exposing you to personal financial ruin.

How a Bad Faith Lawyer Builds Your Case

The Workflow of Legal Representation

If you are exploring if you can sue an insurance company, an experienced lawyer will systematically build a case against the insurer. The flow typically involves:

  • Policy review: Meticulously analyzing your contract language.
  • Evidence collection: Gathering claim files, adjuster notes, and independent estimates.
  • Insurer behavior analysis: Comparing the adjuster's actions against state insurance regulations and industry standards.
  • Legal claim: Drafting the necessary notices and complaints required by Florida courts.

Settlement vs Lawsuit

In many instances, simply hiring a lawyer forces the insurance company to re-evaluate their position. A lawyer can often negotiate a fair settlement before trial. However, if the insurer remains stubborn, your attorney will file a formal lawsuit and prepare for litigation.

Steps to File a Bad Faith Insurance Claim

Filing a bad faith lawsuit is a structured process. While trying to figure out how to sue a car insurance company without a lawyer is possible for small disputes, bad faith claims almost always require professional legal counsel.

  1. Review the policy: Confirm coverage details.
  2. Document insurer actions: Save every email, letter, and note from phone calls.
  3. Gather communications: Compile a clear timeline of the delay or denial.
  4. File complaint: Submit a Civil Remedy Notice (CRN) with the state.
  5. Send demand letter: Formally demand full payment and outline the bad faith acts.
  6. Hire a lawyer: Retain counsel experienced in bad faith litigation.
  7. File lawsuit: If the 60-day CRN window expires without a cure, file the suit.
  8. Discovery: Exchange evidence with the insurer's legal team.
  9. Settlement or trial: Resolve the case through negotiation, mediation, or a jury verdict.

Cost of Hiring a Bad Faith Insurance Lawyer

Contingency Fees Explained

Most bad faith attorneys operate on a contingency fee basis. This means you pay nothing upfront. The lawyer's fee is a percentage of the final settlement or court award. If you lose the case, you do not owe attorney fees. This structure allows policyholders to fight massive corporations without financial risk.

Additional Legal Costs

While attorney fees are contingent, there may be out-of-pocket costs for court filing fees, expert witnesses, and obtaining records. Your lawyer should explain how these costs are handled and whether they are deducted before or after the contingency fee is calculated.

How Long Bad Faith Cases Take

Because these cases involve extensive discovery regarding corporate practices, they rarely resolve quickly.

What Affects Case Duration

  • The insurer's willingness to settle.
  • The complexity of the original claim.
  • The speed of the local Jacksonville court dockets.
  • Whether the case requires a full jury trial.

Benefits and Risks

Benefits of Filing a Bad Faith Claim

Filing a claim can yield compensation for your original damages, attorney fees, emotional distress, and sometimes punitive damages meant to punish the insurer.

Risks and Challenges

These cases require patience. Insurance companies employ aggressive defense firms. Without robust documentation and legal representation, proving bad faith is notoriously difficult.

Florida Bad Faith Insurance Laws

Statute of Limitations

In Florida, the statute of limitations for filing a first-party bad faith claim is generally five years from the date the insurer breached the contract, though specific circumstances can alter this. Always consult an attorney immediately.

Jacksonville Court Process

If filing in Duval County, your attorney will guide you through the local Circuit Court procedures, ensuring all state-mandated pre-suit requirements (like the CRN) are strictly followed.

Signs You Need a Bad Faith Lawyer

Not every denied claim equals bad faith. However, you should consult an attorney if:

  • Claim denied unfairly: The reason provided contradicts plain policy language.
  • Repeated delays: The insurer constantly asks for the same documents or ignores your calls.
  • Low settlement offers: The amount offered doesn't even cover undisputed repair costs.

When You May Not Need One

If the dispute is over a minor calculation error or a few hundred dollars, a bad faith lawsuit is not cost-effective. A simple demand letter might suffice.
