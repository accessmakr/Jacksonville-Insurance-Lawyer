What It Means to Sue an Insurance Company

When an insurance company issues a policy, they form a legally binding contract with you. If they fail to uphold their end of the bargain by unjustly denying a claim or paying less than you deserve, you have the legal right to sue your insurance company for the damages.

Lawsuit vs. Complaint

A lawsuit is a formal civil action filed in a court of law asking a judge or jury to force the insurer to pay what is owed, plus potential extra damages. A complaint, on the other hand, is an administrative grievance filed with the Florida Department of Financial Services or a regulatory body. While a complaint can trigger an investigation, only a lawsuit results in a court-ordered financial judgment.

What Is Insurance Bad Faith?

Insurance bad faith occurs when an insurer acts unreasonably or unfairly in handling a policyholder's claim. By law, insurers owe you a duty of "good faith and fair dealing." Examples of bad faith include:

  • Failing to conduct a prompt, thorough, and objective investigation.
  • Denying a claim without providing a reasonable basis.
  • Misrepresenting policy language to avoid paying a claim.

Common Reasons Claims Lead to Lawsuits

Most insurance lawsuits stem from three primary actions taken by the adjuster:

  • Denial: Flatly rejecting a valid claim that falls under your policy coverage.
  • Delay: Stalling the investigation or payment process for months to pressure you into dropping the claim.
  • Underpayment: Offering a lowball settlement that fails to cover the actual cost of your damages or injuries.

How It Works: Legal Basis for Suing an Insurer

Contract Law vs. Bad Faith Law

When you sue, your lawyer will typically base the lawsuit on two main legal pillars. Breach of Contract argues that the insurer violated the specific terms outlined in your policy documents. Bad Faith Law goes a step further, arguing that the insurer's actions were so unreasonable that they violated their statutory duty. Winning a bad faith claim can result in punitive damages beyond the original claim value.

Settlement vs. Trial

It is a common misconception that suing means you will automatically go to court. In reality, the vast majority of insurance lawsuits end in a settlement before a trial ever begins. Filing the lawsuit often acts as leverage, forcing the insurer to the negotiation table to avoid the high costs and public record of a jury trial.

Step-by-Step Process to Sue an Insurance Company

If you are planning how to sue your insurance company, following a structured process is crucial to building a strong case.

  1. Review your policy: Carefully read your declarations page and policy exclusions to confirm coverage.
  2. Gather evidence: Collect photos, repair estimates, medical records, and all correspondence with the adjuster.
  3. File an internal appeal: Exhaust the insurer's internal review process before escalating to a lawsuit.
  4. Send a demand letter: Formally state your claim, the evidence, and the amount demanded.
  5. File a regulatory complaint (optional): Notify the state insurance department to document the issue.
  6. Hire a lawyer or proceed pro se: Consider hiring a bad faith insurance lawyer or deciding if you can manage small claims court alone.
  7. File the lawsuit in court: Your attorney files a formal complaint with the appropriate civil court and serves the insurer.
  8. Discovery process: Both sides exchange documents, take depositions, and build their arguments.
  9. Negotiation and Mediation: Attorneys for both sides meet to attempt a settlement.
  10. Trial or Settlement: The case concludes either with an agreed payout or a judge/jury verdict.

How Much It Costs to Sue an Insurance Company

Lawyer Contingency Fee Structure

Most insurance dispute attorneys work on a contingency fee basis. This means you pay $0 upfront. The lawyer only gets paid if they win your case, taking a percentage (typically between 33% and 40%) of the final settlement or court award.

Hidden Costs and Filing Fees

While you may not pay attorney fees upfront, lawsuits carry other costs. These include court filing fees ($200-$400), costs to formally serve the defendant, and expenses for expert witnesses (like medical experts or structural engineers) who testify on your behalf. Most contingency lawyers cover these costs upfront and deduct them from the final settlement.

How Long It Takes to Sue an Insurance Company

An insurance lawsuit is rarely a quick process. Resolving a case can take anywhere from a few months to several years. A simple breach of contract that settles early might wrap up in 6 to 9 months. However, a complex bad faith case that goes to trial can stretch from 2 to 3 years.

Factors That Affect the Timeline

  • Complexity of the case: Multi-million dollar commercial claims take longer than basic residential claims.
  • Availability of Evidence: Clear-cut evidence speeds up negotiations.
  • Court Backlog: The schedule of the local civil court system can significantly delay trial dates.

Pros and Cons of Suing an Insurance Company

Pros: Accountability and Higher Payouts

The primary benefit of filing a lawsuit is the potential for a higher payout. Adjusters often hold back their best offers until litigation forces their hand. Additionally, suing holds massive corporations accountable, discouraging them from preying on other policyholders.

Risks: Time, Cost, and Stress

The downsides include the immense time commitment and the emotional stress of dealing with depositions and legal jargon. While contingency agreements protect you from upfront legal costs, if you lose at trial, you may be responsible for the insurer's court costs depending on jurisdiction rules.

Florida Laws on Insurance Lawsuits

Florida has specific statutes governing insurance disputes. Under the Florida Bad Faith Law (Florida Statutes Section 624.155), policyholders must file a Civil Remedy Notice (CRN) with the state, giving the insurer 60 days to cure the bad faith before a lawsuit can be officially filed.

Statute of Limitations

In Florida, the Statute of Limitations dictates how long you have to file a claim. Typically, you have five years to sue for a breach of a written contract (like an insurance policy) and two years to file a negligence claim. Missing these deadlines permanently bars you from recovery.

Jacksonville Court Process

If you are in the Jacksonville area, your lawsuit will likely be filed in the Fourth Judicial Circuit Court in Duval County. Local judges and procedures can influence the speed and handling of the discovery and mediation phases.

When You Should Hire an Insurance Lawyer

You should absolutely consult legal counsel if your claim involves a high dollar amount and the insurer issues a total denial, offers a massive underpayment, or subjects you to an endless delay without cause.

When You Can Sue Without a Lawyer

In small claims court, policyholders often proceed without a lawyer. If your dispute is for a minor amount (typically under $8,000 in Florida) and the facts are simple and undisputed, paying an attorney's contingency fee might not make financial sense.
